Objectives

As the COVID-19 virus overtook the world, many people found themselves worried and fearful for their and their loved ones' lives. The race for a vaccine to alleviate the illness began immediately, and people anxiously waited to get their hands on one. At the time that this project was in the works, there were not enough vaccines to go around. Our team wanted to focus on providing vaccine information to those in the state of Georgia where we live as various states provide such a feature that Georgia was, at the time, lacking. Our app seeks to ease the process of determining whether vaccines are available in the vicinity of people and where they can register to get one.

Research

User Research

We started researching users that this app would benefit, namely anyone who would be eligible to receive a COVID-19 vaccine within the next few months. Between a Google Form survey, four user interviews, and information gathered from social media and the news, we quickly pulled together features that people were looking for to alleviate the lack of communication about vaccines. In a perfect world, users wanted an app that would state who was eligible for the vaccine, with information about the different vaccines offered (Pfizer, Moderna, Johnson & Johnson), and ways to search for and be notified of vaccine availability at nearby venues. This was extremely important at the time because, according to the 21 surveys we received, over 90% of these users had not been inoculated against the virus by means of a vaccine yet.

Competitive Analysis

Of the five direct competitors and two indirect that we looked at, we started with the Center for Disease Control and Prevention. More commonly known as the CDC, all of our participants at this stage, including those who filled out our survey, listed the CDC as the top organization to trust with matters concerning COVID-19. To understand what information was being offered to Georgian residents, we also chose to look at Georgia's Department of Public Health, local healthcare providers, and pharmaceutical clinics (WellStar, Walgreens, and Publix). Of the features made by the companies and government entities we studied, vaccine qualification status, general information about COVID, and vaccine locators were all used.

Our two indirect competitors, Virginia's COVIDwise app and Rhode Island's Crush COVID app, included features that we chose not to build on (namely contact tracing), but their respective user insights helped guide how we designed the funtionality of our site for users.

User Pain Points

Through our individual discussions with users and our 21 survey results, we discovered how, as a whole, users struggled to find vaccine locations if they did not have either their workplace advocating on their behalf or their general practitioner to take the initial step in registering them for the first vaccine. Places such as Walgreens, CVS, and Publix only had enough vaccines available for a set number of the public and healthcare practitioners like WellStar would only register patients in groups to ensure there was enough vaccine for both doses for a single individual. Therefore, the main user pain point centered around supply and demand.






Definition

Research Data Analysis

Users were having a hard time finding clinics with the vaccine, making appointments, and determining which vaccine they would receive. Since users should not have to waste time trying to find information and sign up to be vaccinated, we decided to help by simplifying the location and registration process.

By allowing users to search by county and zip code, notifying them when vaccines are available in their area and when they themselves are eligible to get one, supplying information regarding COVID and the different vaccines available, and registering for a vaccine, our product could potentially increase public health awareness of the COVID-19 vaccine and get more people vaccinated.


User Persona


Marie Johnson

Marie Johnson, a middle-aged mother of two, wants to keep her family safe while, simultaneously, get back to her life pre-pandemic. Her first grandchild was born during COVID and she has yet to meet them for fear of contracting the illness or, worse, passing germs to the newborn. Ultimately, she wants a vaccine without the headache of signing up for one.






Ideation

User Flow

Users discussed preferences for app features during user research, including a notification process for places providing the vaccine nearby, information about vaccines available, and searching for locations through zipcode, GPS, and county. Users like Marie should, in our view, be able to find and save locations that they want updates on.

Similar to job boards, this app is designed to allow users to choose and remove notifications on vaccine availability as they see fit. They can also learn more about the Pfeizer, Johnson & Johnson, and Moderna vaccines through videos, statements from the CDC, and so on.





User Journey Map

Marie isn't as skilled as she would like to be regarding technology. However, she wants to avoid being on hold with the local vaccine distributor for over six hours too. Therefore, she is resolved to look outside of the box. She stumbles across the Georgia COVID Crusher app through the Google Play store and downloads it; there, she chooses to manually look up her county and finds six distributors nearby.

Marie saves three and signs up for frequent email notifications. When one of her saved locations opens COVID-19 vaccine appointments, she receives an email prompting her to sign up, which she does. Within a week, she has her first vaccine and goes on to register for her second with the location itself.

Testing

User Testing

We conducted two rounds of user testing; four users tested our paper prototype and two tested our mid-fidelity app. Feedback from these tests focused mostly on aesthetics. For example, the peach color was considered too light, our font of choice ('Georgia') was hard to read in places, and drop shadows made some unclickable icons appear clickable to users. However, we made quick work of these user insights and updated the site to reflect these changes.

“I think it would seem more legit if there was a verification portion listed under the normal information on the page. It would add credibility.”

Sarah, Paper User

“Your landing page appears too busy with too much to read right now. [...] Pick either stars or hearts for the favorites because you can’t have both.”

Nikki, Paper User

"Use a brighter color for the peach to make it pop out more. Also, maybe add a more modern font like 'Roboto' or one without serifs."

Alina, Mid-Fidelity User

Through our user tests, we updated the homepage, removed most of the drop shadows, and fixed the font to be 'Open Sans'. Our color choice, peach, intentially picked for the Peach State, was altered slightly to be seen more. From a user's perspective, however, the site functioned well. We received few comments on where something was or how best to access portions of the site.

Conclusion

To conclude, our team launched this project because there was a need from the public to get COVID-19 vaccination information out quickly. Through our discussions with family and friends, we gathered user pain points that led us to creating a mobile notification app. As this three-week sprint concluded and our group went on to do other projects, the Georgia Department of Public Health launched its own app for finding and notifying Georgians of COVID vaccines nearby. All's well that ends well.
